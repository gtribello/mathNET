Variable separable differential equations : Exercises

Introduction

In the examples below a method for solving the simplest, variable differential equations is introduced. There are then some examples that allow you to practise using this technique.

Example problems

Click on the problems to reveal the solution

Solve the following first order differential equation: $$ \frac{\textrm{d} f(x) }{\textrm{d}x } = -\lambda f(x) \qquad \textrm{with} \qquad f(0)=1 $$

The set of steps we employ in solving this problem are as follows:
  1. We first place all terms in $f(x)$ on the left hand side of the equation and all terms in $x$ on the right hand side. We thus have: $$ \frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{f(x)} = -\lambda \textrm{d}x $$
  2. Next we integrate both sides. Notice that, because of the variable separation, the variable I am integrating over on the right hand side is $f(x)$ and the variable that I am integrating over on the right hand side of the equation is $x$. $$ \int \frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{f(x)} = - \int \lambda \textrm{d}x \qquad \rightarrow \qquad \ln f(x) = - \lambda x + C \qquad \rightarrow \qquad f(x) = Ae^{-\lambda x} $$ There are two things to notice here: [i] Whenever you perform an indefinite integral you must have an additive constant. One could therefore argue that after the integration the left hand side should read $\ln f(x) + B$ and the right hand side should read $-\lambda t + D$. We have combined these two constants into a single constant $C=D-B$, however. [ii] The constant $A$ is equal to $e^{C}$ as in the last step we have taken antilogs of both sides. For the right hand side we thus have: $e^{-\lambda x + C} = e^Ce^{-\lambda x}$
  3. In the final step we use the boundary condition $f(0)=1$ to solve for $A$. We thus have: $$ f(0) = Ae^{0} = 1 \qquad \rightarrow \qquad A \times 1 = 1 \qquad \rightarrow \qquad A=1 $$

Problems for you to try

Exercise 1

Solve the following differential equations. If no boundary condition is provided skip the third step that was described in the worked example.

(i) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} \tan x = f(x)$ (ii) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} + f(x) = 1$ (iii) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = ax(1-x) \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(0)=\frac{1}{2}$
(iv) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = e^{-f(x)} \sin x \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(0)=0 \quad$ (v) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = x\tan f(x)$ (vi) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = 3 - 2f(x) \quad \textrm{with} \quad y(0)=1$
(vii) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{\ln x}{2f(x)} \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(1) = 0$ (viii) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{f(x)}{x}$
(x) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = g - kf(x) \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(0)=0$ (xi) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{f(x)+1}{x-1}$ (xii) $[f(x)]^2 \frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = x \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(0)=1$
(xiii) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = e^{2x+y} \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(0)=0$ (xiv) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{xf(x)}{x+1}$
(xvi) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = -2x \tan f(x)$ (xvii) $(1+x^2) \frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} + xy = 0$
(xx) $\frac{1}{f(x)}\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{x}{x^2 + 1}$ (xxi) $\frac{\textrm{d}f(x)}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{f(x)}{x(x+1)} \quad \textrm{with} \quad f(1) = 3$
